# Sleuth — Kid Flow & Example Answers

A walkthrough of the 5-stage kid experience with **three worked examples** showing what a child sees, what they do, and what a "good" answer looks like at each step.

Audience: designers, copywriters, teachers, and anyone tuning prompts or example content.

---

## The flow at a glance

| Stage | Name (kid-facing) | What the kid does | What success looks like |
|------:|-------------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| 1 | **Ask** | Pick the strongest of 3 sample questions, then write their own | A focused, specific research question |
| 2 | **Judge sources** | For each candidate source: tap **✓ Trust it** or **✗ Don't trust** | Trust calls roughly match the coach's |
| 3 | **Triangulate** | For each fact, check 2+ sources to see if the fact really appears there | Each fact verified across ≥ 2 sites = "triangulated" |
| 4 | **Explain** | Rewrite a key fact in their own words (no copy-paste) | Graded **Exceeding / Meeting / Approaching / Far from** |
| 5 | **Spot the issue** | Fact-check AI-written answers, label each as **Accurate / Wrong fact / AI tells / Both** | Catch errors and AI-hallucination tells |

Each stage builds on the last. The session's notepad carries the topic, sources, facts, and the kid's answers forward.

## Example 1 — "Why do octopuses change color?"

### Stage 1 — Ask

**Coach offers 3 sample questions:**

| Choice | Quality | Question |
|:---:|:---:|---|
| A | bad | *"Octopus."* |
| B | ok | *"What do octopuses do?"* |
| C | **strong** | *"Why do octopuses change color, and how do they do it?"* |

**Kid picks:** C — *"Nice eye!"*

**Then writes their own question:**
> *"Why do octopuses change the color of their skin so fast?"*

**Coach critique:** ✨ Strong — *"You named a specific thing (color change) and asked HOW + WHY. That's a researcher question."*

### Stage 2 — Judge sources

| Source | Domain | Kid's call | Coach's call |
|---|---|:---:|:---:|
| *National Geographic Kids — Octopus camouflage* | natgeokids.com | ✓ Trust | ✓ Trust |
| *"10 CRAZY OCTOPUS FACTS!!" — blog post* | crazyseastuff.blog | ✗ Don't trust | ✗ Don't trust |
| *Monterey Bay Aquarium — Cephalopod page* | montereybayaquarium.org | ✓ Trust | ✓ Trust |
| *"Why I think octopuses are aliens" — Reddit comment* | reddit.com | ✓ Trust | ✗ Don't trust |

**Score:** 3 / 4 — coach gently flags the Reddit one: *"Random forum posts aren't the same as experts. Look for who wrote it."*

### Stage 3 — Triangulate

For each of 5 facts pulled by the coach, the kid clicks each source and says **yes / no, the fact is on this site**.

**Fact 1:** *"Octopuses have special cells called chromatophores that change color."*
- NatGeo Kids → **yes** ✓ verified
- Monterey Bay → **yes** ✓ verified
- → **Triangulated (2/2 sites)** 🎉

**Fact 2:** *"Octopuses can taste with their suckers."*
- NatGeo Kids → **yes** ✓ verified
- Monterey Bay → **yes** ✓ verified
- → **Triangulated (2/2 sites)** 🎉

**Fact 3:** *"Octopuses have three hearts."* — verified on 2/2 sites. Triangulated.

**Final summary:** **3 out of 5 facts triangulated** → **Triangulator badge unlocked** 🏅

### Stage 4 — Explain

**Coach picks one triangulated fact:**
> *"Octopuses have special cells called chromatophores that change color."*

**Kid writes in their own words:**
> *"Octopuses have tiny color bags in their skin called chromatophores. When the octopus squeezes them, different colors show up — that's how they hide so fast."*

**Grade:** ✨ **Exceeding** — *"You used the science word AND explained what it actually does. You taught it back."*

### Stage 5 — Spot the issue

The coach shows 3 AI-written answers to *"Why do octopuses change color?"* The kid labels each:

| AI answer | Kid's label | Correct label |
|---|---|---|
| *"Octopuses change color using chromatophores in their skin."* | **Accurate** | ✅ Accurate |
| *"Octopuses change color because they get embarrassed when divers see them."* | **Wrong fact** | ✅ Wrong fact |
| *"As a large language model, I cannot personally observe octopuses, but…"* | **AI tells** | ✅ AI tells |

**Mission complete.** Coach summary: *"You asked a real question, judged your sources, triangulated 3 facts, taught one back, and caught an AI making stuff up. That's the whole research loop."*

## Example 2 — "How do bees make honey?"

### Stage 1 — Ask

| Choice | Quality | Question |
|:---:|:---:|---|
| A | bad | *"Bees."* |
| B | **strong** | *"How do bees turn flower nectar into honey?"* |
| C | ok | *"Why are bees important?"* |

**Kid picks:** A — *"Almost — look again. B is the strongest because it names two things (nectar → honey) and asks HOW."*

**Kid's own question (after seeing the reveal):**
> *"How do bees make honey from flowers?"*

**Coach critique:** ✓ Meeting — *"Good and clear. You could go further by asking what changes inside the bee or in the hive."*

### Stage 2 — Judge sources

| Source | Kid | Coach |
|---|:---:|:---:|
| *USDA — Honey Bee Basics (gov PDF)* | ✓ | ✓ |
| *honey-secrets-revealed.online* | ✓ | ✗ |
| *BBC Earth — Inside the Hive* | ✓ | ✓ |
| *Kid science magazine — Highlights* | ✓ | ✓ |

**Coach note on the .online site:** *"Look at the address. `.gov` and big science sites are usually safer than ones that sound like clickbait."*

### Stage 3 — Triangulate

**Fact 1:** *"Bees collect nectar from flowers using a long tongue called a proboscis."*
- USDA → **yes** ✓
- BBC → **yes** ✓
- Highlights → **yes** ✓
- → **Triangulated 3/3** 🎉

**Fact 2:** *"Bees store nectar in a special stomach called a 'honey stomach'."* — Triangulated 2/3.

**Fact 3:** *"Worker bees fan the nectar with their wings to dry it into honey."* — Triangulated 3/3.

**Score:** 4 / 5 facts triangulated.

### Stage 4 — Explain

**Fact to explain:** *"Bees store nectar in a 'honey stomach' separate from their regular stomach."*

**Kid writes:**
> *"Bees have two stomachs. One is for food they eat. The other is just for nectar — it's like a backpack inside the bee."*

**Grade:** ✓ **Meeting** — *"Good analogy with the backpack. To exceed, also name WHY two stomachs (so the nectar stays clean and gets mixed with bee enzymes)."*

### Stage 5 — Spot the issue

| AI answer | Kid | Correct |
|---|---|---|
| *"Bees make honey by drying nectar with their wings and adding enzymes."* | Accurate | ✅ Accurate |
| *"Bees make honey by mixing pollen with water in their feet."* | Wrong fact | ✅ Wrong fact |
| *"I'm an AI assistant, so I haven't seen a hive myself, but here's what sources say…"* | AI tells | ✅ AI tells |
| *"Bees produce honey using their tongues, and as an AI I should mention this is well documented."* | **Both** | ✅ Both (the AI-tell + tongue-only is also a wrong-fact framing) |

## Example 3 — "Why is the sky blue?"

A classic — chosen to show what **misconceptions** look like at each stage.

### Stage 1 — Ask

| Choice | Quality | Question |
|:---:|:---:|---|
| A | ok | *"What color is the sky?"* (too simple) |
| B | bad | *"Sky stuff."* |
| C | **strong** | *"Why does the sky look blue during the day but orange at sunset?"* |

**Kid picks:** A — *"Almost — look again. A is too easy. C asks WHY and notices TWO things (day vs. sunset). That's a researcher's question."*

**Kid's own:**
> *"Why is the sky blue and not green?"*

**Coach critique:** ✨ Strong — *"Nice — you compared it to another color, which means you're already thinking like a scientist."*

### Stage 2 — Judge sources

| Source | Kid | Coach |
|---|:---:|:---:|
| *NASA SpacePlace — Why is the sky blue?* | ✓ | ✓ |
| *Quora answer by anonymous user* | ✓ | ✗ |
| *Met Office (UK) — Sky colors explained* | ✓ | ✓ |
| *YouTube comment screenshot* | ✗ | ✗ |

**Coach note:** *"Quora can be helpful, but anyone can post. NASA and the Met Office are scientists who study this for their job."*

### Stage 3 — Triangulate

**Fact 1:** *"Sunlight is made of all the colors mixed together."*
- NASA → **yes** ✓
- Met Office → **yes** ✓
- → Triangulated 2/2 🎉

**Fact 2:** *"Air molecules scatter blue light more than other colors."*
- NASA → **yes** ✓
- Met Office → **yes** ✓
- → Triangulated 2/2 🎉

**Fact 3:** *"At sunset, light travels through more air so blue scatters away and orange/red is left."* — Triangulated 2/2.

**Score:** 3 / 3 — Triangulator badge 🏅

### Stage 4 — Explain

**Fact to explain:** *"Air molecules scatter blue light more than other colors."*

**Kid writes (first try):**
> *"The sky is blue because of the ocean reflecting up."*

**Grade:** ✗ **Far from** — *"That's a famous myth! Your sources didn't say that. Try again — what did NASA say air molecules do to blue light?"*

**Kid writes (second try):**
> *"Sunlight has every color. When it hits the air, the blue color bounces around the most, so blue gets spread all over the sky. That's why we see blue."*

**Grade:** ✨ **Exceeding** — *"You corrected your own mistake AND used the science. Real researchers do this."*

> 💡 *This is the most important moment in the whole flow — Stage 4 catches misconceptions that look fine on the surface.*

### Stage 5 — Spot the issue

| AI answer | Kid | Correct |
|---|---|---|
| *"The sky is blue because air molecules scatter blue light more than other colors."* | Accurate | ✅ Accurate |
| *"The sky is blue because it reflects the ocean."* | Wrong fact | ✅ Wrong fact |
| *"I don't have personal experience seeing the sky, but according to sources…"* | AI tells | ✅ AI tells |

**Mission complete.** Coach: *"You started with a guess, found out you were wrong, fixed it with your sources, and then spotted the same myth when an AI said it. That's the whole point."*

## Design notes for content authors

### What makes a Stage 1 "strong" question?
- Names **one specific thing** (not a whole topic).
- Asks **WHY** or **HOW** (not just WHAT).
- Compares, contrasts, or notices something surprising.

### What makes a Stage 2 source "trustable" for kids?
- Author or organization is visible and known (museum, gov, university, kid-science publisher).
- URL is calm, not hype-y. `.gov`, `.edu`, `.org` of known orgs > `.online`, `.blog`, random `.com`.
- Has dates, citations, or named authors.

### Stage 3 "triangulated" = 2+ trusted sources agree
- One source saying a fact = not enough.
- Two sources from different orgs saying the same thing = trustable.
- Three+ = confident.

### Stage 4 grading rubric
- **Exceeding** — own words, uses the science term, also explains *why* or gives an analogy.
- **Meeting** — own words, fact is correct, complete enough to teach back.
- **Approaching** — fact correct but vague or partly copy-pasted feel.
- **Far from** — myth, misconception, or unrelated.

### Stage 5 — common "AI tells"
- *"As an AI / large language model…"*
- *"I don't have personal experience but…"*
- *"According to sources I was trained on…"*
- Over-hedging / over-confident with no source.
- Mixing a real fact with a confident myth.

## Tuning the example bank

Each topic in the example bank should provide:
- 3 candidate questions (1 bad, 1 ok, 1 strong) for Stage 1.
- 3–5 candidate sources (mix of trust/don't-trust) for Stage 2.
- 5 candidate facts with at least 3 that **can** be triangulated across the trusted sources, for Stage 3.
- 1 anchor fact for the kid to explain in Stage 4.
- 3–4 AI-written answers for Stage 5 (1 accurate, 1 wrong-fact, 1 AI-tell, optionally 1 both).

Keep topics concrete, observable, and kid-curiosity-bait: animals, weather, food, space, dinosaurs, plants, the body.
